> How I identify what goes unsaid in a seemingly reasonable argument

Today I came across a video of [Elon Musk being interviewed by CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/05/16/elon-musk-work-from-home-morally-wrong-when-some-have-to-show-up.html). Discussing remote work, he argued, in essence, that many people have to show up in person—people who manufacture cars, prepare and deliver food, or make house calls to perform repairs. If they cannot work from home, why should people who sit at computers be allowed to? In his view, this was not merely a question of productivity but a moral issue.

When I first heard this, I was surprised by how convincing it sounded.

It appealed to a very basic sense of fairness: some people commute every day and must be physically present at work, while others get to stay home. If the degree of hardship involved in their work differs so much, are the latter benefiting at the former’s expense?

But after I closed the video, something still felt off.

I began asking myself: **Why did I find this argument persuasive? Does its conclusion really follow naturally from the facts?**

## It Sounds Fair

Musk’s argument is compelling because it does not directly challenge the efficiency of remote work. Instead, it frames the issue in terms of “fairness.”

No one wants to come out against fairness. Once we accept that frame, it is easy to follow his reasoning to its apparent conclusion: if some people cannot work remotely, then those who can should not be allowed to do so either.

But I later realized that a subtle shift in meaning had taken place.

The fairness he invokes is actually one very specific kind of fairness:

> Fairness means subjecting everyone to the same restrictions.

But that is not the only possible meaning of fairness.

Fairness could also mean allowing different arrangements for different working conditions. Or it could mean that when a benefit cannot yet be made available to everyone, those who can benefit from it should be allowed to do so while we look for ways to extend that possibility to more people.

The dispute is not about whether we want fairness. It is about **which kind of fairness we should pursue.**

## What If We Change the Example?

To test whether the argument held up, I kept its structure intact and changed only the examples:

- Some workplaces cannot have air conditioning, so no one else should be allowed to use it.
- Some people do not receive paid leave, so those who already have it should give it up.
- Some illnesses cannot be diagnosed remotely, so people who can consult a doctor online should not use telemedicine.

Once the examples change, the logic that initially seemed “fair” suddenly feels strange.

We do not usually believe that the way to eliminate inequality is to take away improvements that some people already enjoy. A more reasonable approach would be to preserve those improvements while looking for ways to give more people comparable convenience, compensation, or choice.

Technological progress has never unfolded uniformly. The internet, smartphones, automation, and telemedicine all benefited some people first, then gradually spread. If “this cannot benefit everyone immediately” means “no one should be allowed to use it,” then almost no progress could ever begin.

None of this means that remote work is inherently right or that every job is suited to it. Companies can still discuss collaboration efficiency, management costs, information security, and the nature of their business. But those are questions about how work gets done and how organizations are designed. The fact that some people must work on-site does not, by itself, justify the leap to the claim that it is immoral for others to work remotely.

## The Real Point of Dispute Is the Part Left Unsaid

When I broke the argument down again, I found that it consisted of roughly three steps:

1. Some jobs must be performed on-site.
2. If a work arrangement cannot be offered to everyone, offering it only to some people is unfair.
3. Therefore, remote work is morally problematic.

The first step is a factual claim, and the third is the conclusion. What actually determines that conclusion is the second step:

> **If a benefit cannot be available to everyone, no one should be allowed to have it.**

That is not a fact. It is a value premise.

Accept it, and the conclusion seems perfectly natural. Reject it, and the entire argument must be reconsidered.

The “logical trap” I identified is not a simple lie, nor is it necessarily a formal fallacy with a fixed label. It is more like a rhetorical technique: hide the most controversial value judgment in the middle of the argument, present only an easily accepted fact and a conclusion that sounds righteous, and let the audience’s mind supply the missing bridge.

That invisible bridge is often the very thing we most need to examine.

## Who Chooses the Standard of Comparison?

This reminded me of another common line in the workplace:

> We don’t follow the 996 schedule like some other companies do.

The statement may be factually accurate. The problem is that it chooses the employees’ point of comparison for them.

When a company compares its working arrangements with 996, its existing overtime no longer looks so bad. But what employees may really want to know is: why not compare the company with those that genuinely respect work-life balance?

Musk’s argument about remote work differs in form from this workplace claim, but both have a similar effect: they quietly dictate how we should make the comparison.

The former compares people who can work remotely with those who cannot, making convenience look like a moral debt. The latter compares intense work with even more intense work, making harm look like a benefit.

Once we accept someone else’s chosen standard of comparison, the conclusion that follows can easily begin to seem self-evident.

## What I Really Learned Was Not Whether We Should Work Remotely

Looking back, the most important thing I took from the interview was not a position on WFH, but a concrete exercise in thinking.

The next time I hear something that sounds “especially reasonable,” I hope I can resist the urge to support or oppose it immediately. Instead, I want to pause and ask a few questions:

- Is this statement describing a fact or expressing a value?
- What premise has been omitted between the fact and the conclusion?
- If I preserve the argument’s structure but substitute a different example, does it still hold?
- Whom has the speaker chosen as the point of comparison, and why that particular one?
- Is there another, equally reasonable set of values that could also explain these facts?

Critical thinking does not mean habitually disagreeing with others, nor does it mean finding a name for the “fallacy” in every statement. It is more like a form of patience: even when a statement seems perfectly seamless, we remain willing to pause and ask whether something in the middle has quietly been left out.

Musk did not change my view of remote work.

But he reminded me that the part of an argument most worth questioning is often not what it says, but what it leaves unsaid.

Perhaps critical thinking truly begins with one simple question:

> **Why?**
